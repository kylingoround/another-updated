# Introduction

I led the product design and UI / UX design for an A.I.-assisted cybersecurity dashboard that helps security operators monitor and analyst online traffics.

When I joined the team, there was a big divide between Graphen’s technical solutions and users' day-to-day lives. I led the end-to-end product design process from technical solutions to end execution.

# PROBLEM

How A.I. can help with cybersecurity problems? Finding an answer was my starting point. It gave us some goals to strive for.
Cybersecurity operators suffer from long work hours and stress from high stakes while the industry is suffering from unmet workforce demands. Hackers are constantly improving their attack methods and don’t follow a 9-to-6 schedule, but A.I. can help with those problems by being constantly vigilant, and being able to adapt to new types of attacks.

# USER CONTEXT AND PAIN POINTS

I worked with stakeholders, project managers, cybersecurity practitioners (users) and external experts to define user-flows, discover key personas and summarize their pain points.
There are two roles: junior operators and senior analysts. Junior operators will conduct initial screenings and report suspicious anomalies to analysts who will trace the origins of the anomalies, verify if they are indeed threats instead of false alerts, and create a plan to stop or defend against possible attacks.

# CHALLENGE

Remember that Security Officer is a tough job with high stakes? That means low margin of error and they can’t trust a machine unless it is 100% accurate. This creates a challenge that even state of the art technology won’t satisfy with user’s demands, and we have to anticipate user’s needs to investigate A.I.’s decision-making process to see if it is right or wrong.

<!-- ![](https://paper-attachments.dropbox.com/s_9FCB84EFBC580CB5605114E61EA103B6902650A4F0886287613E54B90E99A86C_1566179622877_image.png) -->

<Image src='https://paper-attachments.dropbox.com/s_9FCB84EFBC580CB5605114E61EA103B6902650A4F0886287613E54B90E99A86C_1566179622877_image.png'/>

A.I.’s thinking process can be a black box that is hard to read. It sees and learns numbers and their reasoning can be a string of numbers, like 0.76 which roughly translates to 76% confident that they made the right prediction. It was very challenging to solve that require the efforts of the whole industry. Meanwhile, we decided to give our users the ability to investigate the same information that our robot’s decision-making was based on if we give them the right tool to probe and visualize data. Thinking about our A.I. is an alert dog that’s guarding the house. It can smell something far ahead of humans, but they need human to eventually find out what went wrong.

# INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

With the core features laid out, I created the information architecture with project managers. We further examined the feasibility of each feature with details and decided to focus on a dashboard and visualize tools first.
Then I created a low-to-medium fidelity mock-up to discuss details with users, stakeholders, and experts. After reaching a consensus, I started to work on visual design and technical demos of visualizations.

# NEXT STEP

We only worked on a tool to poke the surface of the ocean of data. There are still many opportunities to smarten-up the A.I. to give more responsive and valid predictions, with better explanations.
Once the system is robust enough, the team can focus on bringing a better experience for team members to communicate with each other. Our team also focused on functionality test at the MVP stage. I expected to focus more on usability tests as functions expand and user base grows.
